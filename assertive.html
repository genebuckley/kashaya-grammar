Assertive

 Kashaya
Grammar

The assertive, as it was called by Bob Oswalt, is special kind of verb in Kashaya. It has a minimal or "defective" size, consisting solely of the consonant /ʔ/. It is probably derived historically from the verb stem /ʔi-/ meaning "be". Like that verb, it can be followed by suffixes, but only a restricted set. Because it is not a full verb, it behaves as a clitic and interacts with the preceding word.

The assertive can occur as /ʔ/ at the end of an utterance, where it has the meaning "is, are", or can simply emphasize the truth of an assertion (which gives it this name).

  • balay | balayʔ
    blood | it is blood
  • mu qʼóʔdi ʔ
    he is good

If the assertive follows a sonorant consonant, it will combine with it to make a glottalized sound. After a stop, it creates an ejective.

  • balay | balayʔ
    blood | it is blood
  • muʔtʼaw | muʔtʼawʔ
    to be cooked | it is cooked
  • šeʔeʔ | šeʔetʼ
    a basket | it's a basket
  • simaʔ | simakʼ
    to fall asleep | he (did) fall asleep

More often, the assertive occurs in the middle of a sentence as part of the copula /ʔe·/.

Between consonants, the /ʔ/ is realized as /i/. This situation arises only when the assertive has a suffix that starts with a consonant, and also is placed after a word that ends in a consonant. A common example is the quotative evidential /-do/, which is typical of storytelling. The sequence /ʔ-do/ can occur after any part of the sentence that is emphasized, but often it is simply found near the beginning of the sentence.

  • mi· ṭʼo ʔdo ʔahša baṭʰé· mu·kinʔ duqʰayá·duce·du
    they say he catches a lot of fish there
  • ʔama· šóʔ tʰin ido mu duwi
    Coyote didn't pay attention, they say
  • qʰaʔbe ʔácaʔ yacʼ ido ṭʼí· šiʔba tol qʰaʔbe
    Rock Man's body was all rock (they say)

For more examples of alternations like this, see the discussion of the form /ʔyow/.
